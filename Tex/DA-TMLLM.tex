\chapter{基于扩散增强可信多模态大语言模型的旋转机械抗幻觉故障诊断方法}\label{chap:DA-TMLLM}

\section{引言}\label{sec:DA-TMLLM_introduction}

电动汽车动力系统的传动子系统等旋转机械的稳定运行对于保障车辆安全至关重要。滚动轴承作为此类机械的核心部件，承担减小摩擦和承载负荷等多种关键功能，且通常运行于恶劣环境中 \cite{PU, JUST}。因此，对轴承运行数据进行持续监测并快速诊断故障特征，对于保障安全至关重要。得益于传感器技术、信号采集技术和深度学习技术的发展，数据驱动的旋转机械故障诊断方法已成为主流，其能够基于历史数据自动推导出诊断结果 \cite{10577994, HIT}。
近年来，LLMs 凭借其优异的性能，在故障诊断领域逐步替代 CDLMs \cite{Peng_Liu_Du_Gao_Wang_2025}，能够在多种工况下提供准确的诊断结果与维护建议。
尽管取得了上述进展，LLMs 遭遇“幻觉”而失效的情况仍是一个突出的问题。幻觉指 LLMs 生成的长文本回答中包含事实错误的现象，在故障诊断语境下，具体表现为给出不正确的诊断结果或不恰当的维护建议 \cite{li-etal-2023-halueval, azaria-mitchell-2023-internal}。实际部署时，真实的故障标签通常不可获取，因此难以判定 LLMs 生成的回复是否包含事实错误。LLMs 的幻觉导致的错误信息可能导致严重的经济损失，甚至危及人员安全。LLMs 的幻觉检测非常重要，但当前相关工作在消除事实错误并检测 LLMs 生成回答中的幻觉方面，仍面临以下挑战：

（1）统计偏差引发幻觉。工业滚动轴承在服役周期的绝大多数时间内保持健康状态，导致轴承故障样本集合存在固有的类别不平衡，呈现长尾分布特性。不平衡的训练集使得模型学习到的决策边界发生偏移，并在 LLMs 中引入统计偏差，促使其倾向于预测多数类。因此，样本量丰富的健康类别及发生频率较高的故障模式主导了梯度更新过程，成为偏差的主要来源。在推理阶段，若输入样本属于少数类别，也即尾部类别，模型依然倾向于预测更常见的类别，从而导致罕见但关乎安全的故障被漏报。

（2）非预期输入引发幻觉。部署后，LLMs 可能会遭遇非预期输入，通常是来自未见过的旋转机械类型的传感器信号或其他与领域无关的数据源。面对此类输入时，LLMs 通常会产生幻觉，生成看似流畅实则编造的回答，而非拒绝响应查询。究其原因，当前 LLMs 缺乏对其认知边界的明确认知，无法判断何时应保持沉默。此外，若非预期输入与训练数据存在较强的语义重叠，例如来自新型轴承的信号与已知数据高度相似，LLMs 可能会表现出过度的自信，为该输入分配较高的后验概率。

（3）序列级幻觉具有隐蔽性。对于回答序列中的每个词元，其后验概率主要受模型内部先验、语料库词频以及局部句法模式的影响，而非取决于该词元与训练数据的匹配程度。因此，当检测器仅基于词元级别的后验概率进行判断时，幻觉现象有较大概率逃避检测。此外，在评估整个回答序列时，诸多形式正确但缺乏实质语义的短语，如“\textit{从提供的列表中最合适的轴承状态类型是（the most suitable bearing state type from the provided list is）}”。这样的语言冗余的存在将稀释幻觉内容。最终，即使回答序列中存在幻觉，幻觉量化指标仍可能输出正常值。

针对上述问题，本文提出一种扩散增强的可信多模态大型语言模型（Diffusion-Augmented Trustworthy Multimodal Large Language Model, DA-TMLLM）。
首先，设计一个 ControlNet 引导的跨数据集条件扩散模型（ControlNet-Guided Cross-Dataset Conditional Diffusion Model, CGCDM），缓解由统计偏差引发的幻觉。
其次，通过在多个轴承数据集的文本-信号对上对预训练 LLMs 进行微调，开发可信多模态大型语言模型（Trustworthy Multimodal Large Language Model, TMLLM）。基于所构建的 TMLLM，识别出一个关键词元，设计一种关键词元驱动的不确定性重加权策略，以有效检测序列级幻觉。
最后，设计一种用于检测和缓解幻觉的抗幻觉机制，将 CGCDM 和 TMLLM 整合为统一框架，阐述拒绝非预期输入的流程。DA-TMLLM 的主要贡献如下：

（1）设计 ControlNet 引导的跨数据集条件扩散模型。通过注入可控的语义引导，在保持语义一致性的同时，提升生成样本的多样性。

（2）通过锁定关键词元并设计关键词元驱动的不确定性重加权策略，构建可信多模态大型语言模型，提升 LLMs 的幻觉检测性能。

（3）设计抗幻觉机制，将 CGCDM 与 TMLLM 整合入统一的工作流，并结合非预期输入拒绝方法，在进一步提升检测性能的同时，缓解 LLMs 的幻觉。

（4）在 11 个数据集上的评估结果表明，DA-TMLLM 在 8 项指标上的表现始终优于 11 种对比模型。

\subsection{ControlNet 引导的跨数据集条件扩散模型}

To mitigate inter-dataset discrepancies, we propose a cross-dataset standardization method by applying fixed-duration segmentation, length alignment, and scale normalization.
Then, we develop the architecture of the conditional diffusion model (CDM) to synthesize vibration signals.
Finally, we design a ControlNet to inject controllable semantic guidance into the reverse diffusion phase.

（1）跨数据集信号预处理标准化方法

Since sensor sampling rates, collection conditions, and bearing types vary substantially across different bearing datasets, collected signals exhibit differences on characteristic fault frequency and amplitude.
When these heterogeneous signals are fed directly to a diffusion model, the model tends to treat the inter-dataset discrepancies as random noise, hindering its ability to learn an effective joint distribution.
To address this issue, we propose a cross-dataset standardization method.

In conventional data preprocessing methods, the raw time-domain signal is typically partitioned into fixed-length samples containing an identical number of data points.
Because vibration signals are collected at different sampling rates, the fixed-length partition method shifts the characteristic fault frequencies and thus enlarges inter-dataset discrepancies.
To mitigate this problem, we introduce a fixed-duration segmentation method, and the $m$-th segment is formulated as
\begin{equation}
\bm{x}_o = \bigl\{\, \bm{x}_r[i] \bigm| i = m\nu,\, m\nu+1,\, \dots,\; (m+1)\nu - 1 \bigr\},
\end{equation}
where $\nu$ is the dataset-specific sampling rate, and $\bm{x}_r$ is the raw signal.
After the fixed-duration segmentation, the resulting segments from different datasets should be further aligned in length and amplitude to serve as suitable network inputs.
This is accomplished by successively applying the discrete Fourier cosine transform and scale normalization, denoted as $\operatorname{DFCT}(\cdot)$ and $\operatorname{SNorm}(\cdot)$, respectively.
The $\operatorname{DFCT}(\cdot)$ transforms the time-domain segment into its frequency spectrum, after which only the first $L_{f}$ spectral bins are retained, where $L_{f}$ is a predefined length. To accommodate variations in sampling rate, spectra containing fewer than $L_{f}$ bins are zero-padded, whereas those containing more are truncated.
The amplitude of the resulting spectrum is then mapped to the interval $\left[-1,1\right]$ by $\operatorname{SNorm}(\cdot)$.
% The successive preprocessing process is expressed as
This preprocessing process is expressed as
\begin{equation}
	\label{eq:dcn}
	\bm{x}_v =
	\begin{cases}
		\operatorname{SNorm}\bigl(\operatorname{DFCT}(\bm{x}_o)[0:L_{f}]\bigr),      & \ell \ge L_{f},\\[4pt]
		\operatorname{SNorm}\bigl(\operatorname{Concat}(\operatorname{DFCT}(\bm{x}_o),\bm{0}_{\,L_{f}-\ell})\bigr), & \ell < L_{f},
	\end{cases}
\end{equation}
where $\ell$ is the actual number of spectral bins, and $\operatorname{SNorm}(\cdot)$ is formulated as
\begin{equation}
	\label{eq:scalenorm}
	\operatorname{SNorm}(\bm{x})=g\,\frac{\bm{x}}{\lVert \bm{x}\rVert_{2}},
\end{equation}
where $g=\rho\sqrt{L_{f}}$, and $\rho$ is a predefined scaling factor.

（2）ControlNet 引导的条件扩散生成

We employ a diffusion-based augmentation model that simultaneously promotes training stability and sample diversity, which is composed of a forward diffusion phase and a reverse diffusion phase.
To enhance semantic consistency during generation, we design a ControlNet that injects high-level semantic features into the latent features at every reverse diffusion step.

During the forward diffusion, the preprocessed $\bm{x}_v$ is progressively added with noise through a length-$S$ Markov chain, where isotropic Gaussian noise is incrementally added at each step $s \in \{1,\dots ,S\}$.
The adding noise process at step $s$ is expressed as
\begin{equation}
q(\bm{x}_v^{(s)} \mid \bm{x}_v^{(s-1)}) = \mathcal{N}\!\bigl(\sqrt{\alpha^{(s)}}\,\bm{x}_v^{(s-1)},\, (1-\alpha^{(s)})\bm{I}\bigr),
\end{equation}
where $\bm{I}$ is the identity matrix, $\alpha^{(s)}$ is a noise scaling factor, $\alpha^{(s)} = 1 - \beta^{(s)}$, and  $\beta^{(s)}$ is specified by a predefined variance schedule $\zeta$.
After $S$ steps, $\bm{x}_v^{(S)}$ approaches a pure Gaussian sample distribution.
Using the closed-form accumulation, the whole forward diffusion process can be expressed as
\begin{equation}
q(\bm{x}_v^{(S)} \mid \bm{x}_v^{(0)}) = \mathcal{N}\!\bigl(\sqrt{\hat{\alpha}^{(S)}}\,\bm{x}_v^{(0)},\,(1-\hat{\alpha}^{(S)})\bm{I}\bigr),
\end{equation}
where $\hat{\alpha}^{(S)} = \prod_{s=1}^{S} {\alpha}^{(s)}$.
$\bm{x}_v^{(S)}$ serves as the input for the subsequent reverse diffusion phase.

During the reverse diffusion phase, the initial latent
$\bm{x}_v^{(S)} \sim \mathcal{N}(0,\bm{I})$ is iteratively refined by a step-aware denoiser $\epsilon_{\bm{\theta}_\mathrm{d}}(\cdot)$.
At each diffusion step $s$, the UNet-based noise prediction network receives the triplet
$(\bar{\bm{x}}_v^{(s)},\,s,\,\bm{c})$, estimates the added Gaussian perturbation, and computes the posterior mean
\begin{equation}
	\mu_{{\theta}_\mathrm{d}} \bigl(\bar{\bm{x}}_v^{(s)},\,s,\,\bm{c}\bigr)
	= \frac{\bar{\bm{x}}_v^{(s)} - \sigma^{(s)} \epsilon_{{\theta}_\mathrm{d}} \bigl(\bar{\bm{x}}_v^{(s)}, s, \bm{c}\bigr)}
	{\sqrt{\bar{\alpha}^{(s)}}},
\end{equation}
where $\bar{\alpha}^{(s)} = 1-\bar{\beta}^{(s)}$, $\sigma^{(s)} = \sqrt{1-\bar{\alpha}^{(s)}}$, and $\bm{c}$ is an external condition vector.
$\bm{c}$ supplies a controllable guidance throughout the entire reverse diffusion phase by injecting auxiliary information into the noise prediction network.
A new latent $\bar{\bm{x}}_v^{(s-1)}$ is then sampled from $\mathcal{N} \bigl(
\mu_{{\theta}_\mathrm{d}} \bigl(\bar{\bm{x}}_v^{(s)},\,s,\,\bm{c}\bigr), \bigl(\sigma^{(s)}\bigr)^{2}\bm{I}\bigr).$
Repeating this stochastic reconstruction for all
$s = S,\ldots,1$ progressively suppresses the injected noise, and the final output $\bar{\bm{x}}_v^{(0)}$ converges to the learned data distribution, thereby yielding a genuinely synthesized sample.

To inject controllable semantic guidance into the reverse diffusion phase, we design a ControlNet that embeds auxiliary knowledge as a low-dimensional external condition vector $\bm{c}$.
Specifically, the designed ControlNet is first pre-trained on labeled vibration signals and optimized to map each raw signal to a class-discriminative latent representation.
To build an external condition vector robust to inter-dataset discrepancies, we adopt a triplet input $\bm{H}_{v} = \bigl[\bm{x}_{v},\;\tilde{\bm{x}}_{v},\;\bm{x}_{vr}\bigr]\in\mathbb{R}^{3 \times L_{f}}$, where $\bm{x}_{v}$ is a query sample generated, $\tilde{\bm{x}}_{v}$ is a fault-free signal sample, $\bm{x}_{vr}=\bm{x}_{v}-\tilde{\bm{x}}_{v}$ is a residual sample that amplifies fault-related deviations.
As shown in Fig.~\ref{fig_controlnet}, the encoder of ControlNet consists of three large-kernel convolutional channels followed by three cross-channel attention blocks (CCAB) that fuse features across scales.
Then, the output of encoder is fed into a two-layer fully connected classifier, and the ControlNet is trained by categorical cross-entropy loss.
During diffusion model training, the ControlNet's encoder and classifier weights, denoted as $\bm{\theta}_\mathrm{enc}$ and $\bm{\theta}_\mathrm{cla}$, are kept frozen.

	\centering
	\includegraphics[width=0.8\textwidth]{Img/DA-TMLLM_figures/trans/ControlNet.eps}
	\caption{}{The architecture of ControlNet.}
	\label{fig_controlnet}
\end{figure}

During the reverse diffusion phase, the external condition vector $\bm{c}$ is integrated through a multi-head attention mechanism to ensure semantic consistency in the generated samples.
At the $s$-th reverse diffusion step, the query ($\bm{Q}^{(s)}$), key ($\bm{K}^{(s)}$), and value ($\bm{V}^{(s)}$) representations are computed as
\begin{equation}
	\bm{Q}^{(s)} = \bm{W}_{Q}\bar{\bm{x}}_{v}^{(s)},
	\bm{K}^{(s)} = \bm{W}_{K}{\tau}{(\bm{c})},
	\bm{V}^{(s)} = \bm{W}_{V}{\tau}{(\bm{c})},
\end{equation}
where $\bm{W}_{Q}$, $\bm{W}_{K}$, and $\bm{W}_{V}$ are learnable linear projection matrices, and ${\tau}{(\cdot)}$ denotes an encoder that projects $\bm{c}$ and $\bar{\bm{x}}_{v}^{(s)}$ into the same dimensional feature space.
The attention output is then computed as
\begin{equation}
	\bm{A}^{(s)} = \operatorname{Softmax} \Bigl(\frac{\bm{Q}^{(s)}\bigl({\bm{K}}^{(s)}\bigr)^{\!\top}}{\sqrt{d_{k}}}\Bigr){\bm{V}}^{(s)},
\end{equation}
where $d_k$ is the dimensionality of the keys.
In the multi-head attention framework, the attention process is independently applied across multiple heads.
The outputs from each head are then concatenated and projected through a linear transformation to yield the final output, which is formulated as
\begin{equation}
	\operatorname{MHA}\bigl(\bar{\bm{x}}_{v}^{(s)},\bm{c}\bigr)=
	\bm{W}_{o}\bigl[\bm{A}^{(s)}_{1};\dots;\bm{A}^{(s)}_{N_h}\bigr],
\end{equation}
where $\bm{W}_{o}$ is the output projection matrix and $N_h$ denotes the number of attention heads.
By integrating the external condition vector $\bm{c}$ in this manner, we effectively inject semantic context into each step of the reverse diffusion process.

The loss function of the designed ControlNet-guided cross-dataset conditional diffusion model can be formulated as
\begin{equation}
	L^{(s)}=\mathbb{E}_{\substack{s\sim[1,S],\bm{x}_v^{(s)},\epsilon^{(s)}}}
	\Bigl[\operatorname{Huber}\bigl(\lVert r^{(s)} \bigr)\rVert\bigr)\Bigr],
\end{equation}
where $r^{(s)} = \bigl\lVert\epsilon^{(s)} - \epsilon_{{\theta}_\mathrm{d}}\!\bigl(\bar{\bm{x}}_{v}^{(s)},s,\bm{c}\bigr)\bigr\rVert$, and $\operatorname{Huber}(\cdot)$ is Huber loss.
Huber loss attenuates the influence of large point-wise residuals and is therefore more robust to the phase offsets that may exist between signal samples, which is expanded as
\begin{equation}
	\operatorname{Huber}\bigl(r^{(s)}\bigr)=
	\begin{cases}
		\dfrac{1}{2}\, \bigl(r^{(s)}\bigr)^{2},          & r^{(s)} < \lambda ,\\[4pt]
		\lambda\,\bigl(r^{(s)}-\dfrac{\lambda}{2}\bigr),   & \text{otherwise},
	\end{cases}
	\label{eq:huber}
\end{equation}
where $\lambda$ is the transition threshold of $\operatorname{Huber}(\cdot)$.

TMLLM aims to provide trustworthy diagnostic results and analysis across multiple datasets.
In TMLLM, a multimodal LLM is developed by fine-tuning a pre-trained LLM on text-signal pairs.
To derive trustworthy answers, we target a key token of which energy decreases abnormally by computing point-biserial correlation coefficient and conducting a significance test.
Furthermore, we design a key token-driven uncertainty reweighting strategy to quantify the uncertainty in LLM answer.

（1）基于文本-信号对的大语言模型微调
Since recent pre-trained LLMs are optimized for discrete textual tokens and cannot natively process vibration signals, we introduce a dual-branch tokenizer-encoder block, which converts vibration signal samples and corresponding textual descriptions into signal-specific and token-specific features.
The modality-specific features are subsequently aligned by a text-signal alignment block, and the whole model is fine-tuned to maximize cross-modal alignment.

In the developed dual-branch tokenizer-encoder block, the encoder shares the weight $\bm{\theta}_\mathrm{enc}$ with ControlNet.
Let $\bm{x}_{g}$ be a synthesized sample, the input of the encoder branch can be formulated as
$\bm{H}_{g} = \bigl[\bm{x}_{g},\;\tilde{\bm{x}}_{g},\;\bm{x}_{gr}\bigr] \in \mathbb{R}^{3 \times L_{f}}$, where $\bm{x}_{g}$ is a query sample, $\tilde{\bm{x}}_{g}$ is a fault-free signal sample, and $\bm{x}_{gr}=\bm{x}_{g}-\tilde{\bm{x}}_{g}$ is a residual sample that amplifies fault-related deviations.
This encoder produces signal-specific features for each input.
Let $\bm{D}=\{k_{1},\dots,k_{N_c}\}$ be a collection of short textual fault descriptions, one description per fault category, where $N_c$ denotes the number of categories.
Furthermore, the multimodal input to the TMLLM is obtained by fusing $\bm{H}_{g}$ and $\bm{T}$, and is denoted as $\bm{M}_{g}=\{\bm{H}_{g},\bm{T}\}$.
In the tokenizer branch, we feed $\bm{D}$ through the LLM's native tokenizer $\operatorname{Tok}(\cdot)$ and embedding layer $\operatorname{Emb}(\cdot)$ to obtain a sequence of token-specific features, denoted as $\bm{E}=\operatorname{Emb}(\operatorname{Tok}(\bm{D})) \in \mathbb{R}^{\gamma\times N_c\times h}$, where $h$ is the hidden size of $\operatorname{Emb}(\cdot)$, $\gamma$ is the maximum length of token-specific features.
As illustrated in Fig. \ref{fig_framework}, the weights of the dual-branch tokenizer-encoder block are kept frozen throughout LLM fine-tuning.

To bridge the gap between the signal-specific/token-specific features and LLMs, we develop a trainable text-signal alignment block that fuses signal-specific and token-specific features into language tokens suitable for input to LLMs.
The alignment block is implemented as a three-layer linear multilayer perceptron.
We initialize this alignment module using a combination of the ControlNet's prior weights and token-specific features.
In particular, the weights of the first two linear layers, denoted $\bm{\theta}_\mathrm{l_{1}}$ and $\bm{\theta}_\mathrm{l_{2}}$, are initialized with the weights $\bm{\theta}_\mathrm{cla}$ of the ControlNet's pre-trained classifier.
Furthermore, we flatten the last two dimensions of $\bm{E}$ and use the resulting matrix as the weight of $l_{3}$, formulated
$\boldsymbol{\theta}_{l_{3}} =\operatorname{reshape}\bigl(\bm{E},(\gamma,N_c h)\bigr)
\in\mathbb{R}^{\gamma\times N_c h}$.
In this way, the $i$-th row of $\boldsymbol{\theta}_{l_{3}}$ encodes the token-level embedding of the $i$-th fault description, providing a semantic projection from the signal-specific features to the language tokens.
Finally, the text-signal alignment block embeds the language tokens into a predefined prompt template and outputs $\bm{x}_{pt}$ as the LLM's input.
The weight of text-signal alignment block is denoted as $\boldsymbol{\theta}_\mathrm{ts}=\{\bm{\theta}_\mathrm{l_{1}}, \bm{\theta}_\mathrm{l_{2}}, \bm{\theta}_\mathrm{l_{3}}\}$, and the weight of the pre-trained LLM is denoted as $\boldsymbol{\theta}_\mathrm{llm}$.
To fine-tune the weights of text-signal alignment block and LLM on downstream tasks, we adopt a parameter-efficient approach using low-rank adaptation (LoRA) \cite{20231213775448}.

（2）关键词元定位

To solve the issue of hiddenness of sequence-level hallucinations, we jointly consider the autoregressive generation rule and diagnosis-specific characteristics.
% We hypothesize that key tokens exist that act as sentinel positions that expose imminent uncertainty.
We hypothesize that certain key tokens act as sentinels, signaling imminent uncertainty.
Then, we carry out a point-biserial correlation computation on a held-out corpus to evaluate the hypothesis, comparing the energy of tokens that precede hallucinated answers against matched tokens in factual answers.
Finally, we perform a significance test to show that the decrease in energy is caused by the undesired input rather than by random fluctuation.
The significance test returns $p=3.2779\times10^{-28}$, rejecting the null that low energy tokens occur at random.

When generating diagnostic answers, LLMs autoregressively generate answers by predicting one token at a time and produce a probability distribution over the vocabulary for the next token, which can be formulated as
\begin{equation}
	P\bigl(\mathit{Token}_{k} = w \;\big|\; \mathit{Token}_{<k}\bigr)
	\;=\;
	\frac{\exp\bigl(\operatorname{Proj}(\bm{a}_{k})_{\,w}\bigr)}
	{\displaystyle \sum_{w' \in \mathcal{V}} 
		\exp\bigl(\operatorname{Proj}(\bm{a}_{k})_{\,w'}\bigr)},
\end{equation}
where $\mathit{Token}_{k}$ is the output token at generation step $k$, $\mathit{Token}_{<k}$ is the entire diagnostic answer of tokens generated before $k$, $\mathcal{V}$ is the LLM's native vocabulary set, $w$ is a single token drawn from $\mathcal{V}$, $\bm{a}_{k}$ is the hidden state vector at $k$, $\operatorname{Proj}(\cdot)$ is a learnable linear transformation function, and $\sum_{w\in \mathcal{V}} P\bigl(\mathit{Token}_{k} = w \mid \mathit{Token}_{<k}\bigr) = 1$.
When many tokens have lower probabilities, it indicates higher uncertainty in the next-token prediction.
Furthermore, the negative log-likelihood (NLL) of a token can be interpreted as the  token-level energy, which is formulated as
\begin{equation}
	\operatorname{NLL}{(\mathit{Token}_{k}=w)}
	=
	-\,\log
	\frac{\exp\bigl(\operatorname{Proj}(\bm{a}_{k})_{\,w}\bigr)}
	{\displaystyle\sum_{w' \in \mathcal{V}}
		\exp\bigl(\operatorname{Proj}(\bm{a}_{k})_{\,w'}\bigr)},
\end{equation}
where $\operatorname{NLL}{(\cdot)}$ is the token energy function.
A low NLL means the model assigns a high probability to that token, whereas a high NLL means that the LLM is ambiguous when generating this token.

Based on this analysis, our hypothesis is that when LLM outputs an incorrect fault classification result for the first time, the token energy changes abnormally around the incorrect classification tokens.
This hypothesis is based on two facts.
First, when the model outputs an incorrect fault category in its answer, it is a serious hallucination.
Second, the above analysis about the relationship between hallucination and energy shows that local hallucinations are often accompanied by an unusual change in token energy near the error.
%加一句话阐述什么时候升高什么时候降低

To validate our hypothesis, we first construct a held-out corpus. We use samples from the MBHM dataset \cite{Peng_Liu_Du_Gao_Wang_2025} as normal inputs, use samples from the MaFaulDa dataset \cite{MaFaulDa_dataset} as undesired inputs, and use the Qwen-2 1.5B model \cite{QWEN} as the LLM.
% For normal inputs and undesired input, we generate $N_\mathrm{nor}$ and $N_\mathrm{un}$ answers, respectively.
We generate $N_\mathrm{nor}$ answers for normal inputs and $N_\mathrm{un}$ for undesired inputs.
Moreover, token energies are calculated within a $\pm L_{\mathrm{win}}$ token window centered on the token at which the first misclassification occurs, denoted as $\mathit{Token}_{{\delta}}$.
Then, the point-biserial correlation coefficient, denoted $r_\mathrm{pb}$, is calculated to examine how the token energy relates to the corresponding input.
The point-biserial correlation is the Pearson correlation that fits the special case, quantifying the correlation between binary input type and the continuous token energy.
$r_\mathrm{pb}$ is formulated as
\begin{equation}
	r_\mathrm{{pb}}=
	\frac{\mu_\mathrm{nor} - \mu_\mathrm{un}}{\sigma_\mathrm{total}}
	\sqrt{\frac{N_\mathrm{nor}\,N_\mathrm{un}}{(N_\mathrm{nor}+N_\mathrm{un})^2}},
\end{equation}
where $\mu_\mathrm{nor}$ is the mean token energy for the answers of the normal input, and $\sigma_\mathrm{total}$ is the standard deviation of token energy across all answers.
Finally, we conduct a significance test to evaluate the results of $r_\mathrm{pb}$ to show that the rise in energy is caused by the undesired input rather than by random fluctuation.
The coefficient $r_{pb}$ is converted to a $t$-statistic, which is formulated as
\begin{equation}
	t=r_\mathrm{pb}\sqrt{\frac{(N_\mathrm{nor}+N_\mathrm{un}) - 2}{1 - r_\mathrm{pb}^2}},\,\mathrm{df} = (N_\mathrm{nor}+N_\mathrm{un}) - 2, 
\end{equation}
where $\mathrm{df}$ is the degrees of freedom.
Furthermore, the two-sided $p$-value, denoted as $p_{\text{two-sided}}$, is then obtained from the cumulative distribution function (CDF) of the student-$t$ distribution, which is formulated as
\begin{equation}
	p_{\text{two-sided}} = 2\bigl[1-F_{t_{\mathrm{df}}}(|t|)\bigr]
	= 2P\bigl(T_{\mathrm{df}}\ge |t|\bigr),
	\label{eq:p_value}
\end{equation}
where $F_{t_{\mathrm{df}}}(\cdot)$ is the CDF of the Student-$t$ distribution with $\mathrm{df}$, and $T_{\mathrm{df}}$ is a random variable following this distribution.


As illustrated in Fig. \ref{fig_correlation}, we compute $r_{\mathrm{pb}}$ and $p_{\text{two-sided}}$ between the binary input type and the token-energy sequence within $\pm L_{\mathrm{win}}=3$ centered on $\mathit{Token}_{\delta}$.
At the token $\mathit{Token}_{\delta-1}$, a large effect size is observed, where $|r_{\mathrm{pb}}| > 0.5$ and $r_{\mathrm{pb}} < -0.5$.
The observation indicates that an anomalous decrease in token energy is highly predictive of an undesired input.
Additionally, the significance test returns $p=3\times10^{-28}$, rejecting the null that low energy tokens occur at random.
From a theoretical standpoint, undesired inputs tend to induce localized energy distortions around $\mathit{Token}_{\delta}$.
Because ${\delta}-1$ lies immediately adjacent to ${\delta}$, it is intrinsically more responsive to the earliest signs of undesired inputs, aligning with the statistical evidence.
Accordingly, we adopt $\mathit{Token}_{\delta-1}$ as our key token for undesired input detection, while omitting $\mathit{Token}_{\delta+2}$ despite its higher correlation.

（3）关键词元驱动的不确定性重加权策略

To quantify the uncertainty in LLM outputs and to construct a TMLLM, we propose a key token-driven uncertainty reweighting strategy (KTURS).
The proposed strategy employs token-level energy as an uncertainty quantification metric and assigns greater weights to tokens that exhibit stronger correlation with the designated key token.


For an LLM output $\bm{\hat y}_{g}$ regarding a multimodal input $\bm{x}_{pt}$, denoted as $\bm{\hat y}_{g}=\{\mathit{Token}_{1},\mathit{Token}_{2},\dots,\mathit{Token}_{N_t}\}$, where $N_t$ is the length of output.
We first compute the normalized energy score for each token $\mathit{Token}_{k} (1\leq k\leq N_t)$, which is formulated as
\begin{equation}
	\label{equa:normalized_energy_score}
	\begin{split}
		{\operatorname{\tilde{NLL}}}(\mathit{Token}_k)
		= \,\frac{{\operatorname{NLL}}(\mathit{Token}_k)}
		{\sum_{j=1}^{N_t}{{\operatorname{NLL}}(\mathit{Token}_j)}\,}\,.
	\end{split}
\end{equation}
Then, we derive the token-level uncertainty by enlarging the weight of the key token according to $r_\mathrm{pb}$, denoted as
\begin{equation}
	\label{equa:token_level_uncertainty}
	\begin{split}
		&\operatorname{UT}(\mathit{Token}_k;\omega,r_{\mathrm{pb}})\\
		&= \bigl[1 + (\omega r_{\mathrm{pb}} - 1)\bm{1}_{\{k=\delta-1\}}\bigr]
		\tilde{\operatorname{NLL}}(\mathit{Token}_k),
	\end{split}
\end{equation}
where $\bm{1}_{\{k=\delta-1\}}$ equals 1 when the token index $k=\delta-1$ and 0 otherwise, and $\omega$ is a coefficient to calibrate the degree of token-level uncertainty.
Finally, the uncertainty for the LLM output can be formulated as
\begin{equation}
	\label{equa:overall_uncertainty}
	\operatorname{UA}(\bm{\hat y}_{g})
	= \sum_{k=1}^{N_t}
	\bigl[\,1 + (\omega\,r_{\mathrm{pb}} - 1)\,\bm{1}_{\{k=\delta-1\}}\bigr]\;\tilde{\operatorname{NLL}}(\mathit{Token}_k)\,.
\end{equation}


As shown in Fig. \ref{fig_strategy}, the raw energy scores are first normalized into the uncertainty distribution
$\tilde{\operatorname{NLL}}(\mathit{Token}_k)$, then the uncertainty of the key token $\mathit{Token}_{\delta}$ is amplified by the factor $\omega$, and finally the sequence-level uncertainty $\operatorname{UT}(\mathit{Token}_k;\omega,r_{\mathrm{pb}})$ is obtained by summing the token-level uncertainties over all $N_t$ tokens.

	\caption{}{Illustration of the key token-driven uncertainty reweighting strategy.}

\subsection{基于 DA-TMLLM 的抗幻觉方案}
\label{sec:scheme}

To alleviate and detect LLM hallucinations, the proposed hallucination-resilient scheme integrates the diffusion-based augmentation and the trustworthy multimodal LLM into a unified pipeline.

To alleviate statistical bias-induced hallucinations, the ControlNet-guided cross-dataset conditional diffusion model is designed to produce balanced and semantically aligned synthetic data.
The original and synthetic samples are then fused and used to fine-tune a trustworthy multimodal large language model.  
The pipeline in the training stage is summarized in Algorithm~\ref{alg:training}.
Before any test sample is input, we evaluate the mean and variance of the uncertainty scores on the validation set comprising only normal samples, denoted as $\mu_\mathrm{val}$ and $\sigma_\mathrm{val}$. 
This allows us to define the lower and upper bounds of our uncertainty threshold, $\psi_{lower}=\mu_\mathrm{val} - \sigma_\mathrm{val}$ and $\psi_{upper}=\mu_\mathrm{val} + \sigma_\mathrm{val}$, which together form the decision interval $[\psi_{lower}, \psi_{upper}]$.
Furthermore, we target the key token and design a key token-driven uncertainty reweighting strategy.
The pipeline in the inference stage is summarized in Algorithm~\ref{alg:inference}.

\begin{algorithm}[t]
	\caption{Training Pipeline of Diffusion-Augmented Trustworthy Multimodal LLM}
	\label{alg:training}
	\centering
	\begin{algorithmic}[1]
		\Require Pre-processed dataset $\mathcal{D}_\text{real}=\{(\mathbf{x}_{v},y)\}$, weights $\bm{\theta}_\mathrm{enc}$, ${\theta}_\mathrm{d}$, $\bm{\theta}_\mathrm{cla}$, $\bm{\theta}_\mathrm{ts}$, and $\bm{\theta}_\mathrm{llm}$, hyperparameters $S$, $N_\mathrm{dm}$, $N_\mathrm{llm}$, and $N_{\text{aug}}$, response text $\bm{y}_{g}$
		\Ensure Trained CGCDM $\bm{\theta}_\mathrm{d}^*$, fine-tuned TMLLM $\bm{\theta}_\mathrm{ts}^*$, $\bm{\theta}_\mathrm{llm}^*$
		\Statex \textbf{Stage 1: Train ControlNet-Guided CDM}
		\For{epoch $=1$ \textbf{to} $N_\mathrm{dm}$} \Comment{Training epochs $N_\mathrm{dm}$}
		\ForAll{$(\bm{x}_{v},y)\!\sim\!\mathcal{D}_\text{real}$}
		\State $\bm{x}_{v}^{(S)}\leftarrow\textsc{AddNoise}(\bm{x}_{v},S)$
		\State $L^{(s)}\!\leftarrow\!\lVert\epsilon-\epsilon_{\bm{\theta}_\mathrm{d}}(\bm{x}_{v}^{(s)},y,s)\rVert_2^2$
		\State $\bm{\theta}_\mathrm{d}^*\!\leftarrow\!\bm{\theta}_\mathrm{d}-\eta_{\bm{\theta}_\mathrm{d}}\nabla_{\bm{\theta}_\mathrm{d}}L^{(s)}$
		\EndFor
		\EndFor
		\Statex \textbf{Stage 2: Generate Augmented Samples}
		\For{$n=1$ \textbf{to} $N_{\text{aug}}$} \Comment{Augmented samples $N_{\text{aug}}$}
		\State $\bm x_{v}^{(S)}\sim\mathcal N(\bm 0,\bm I)$ 
		\For{$s=S$ \textbf{downto} $1$}
		\State $\bm c\leftarrow\textsc{ControlNet}(\bm{\theta}_\mathrm{enc}, \bm{\theta}_\mathrm{cla})$
		\State $\bm\mu\leftarrow\textsc{PosteriorMean}(\bm x^{(s)},s,\bm c,\bm{\theta}_\mathrm{d}^*)$
		\State $\bm\bar{\bm x}_{v}^{(s-1)}\sim\mathcal N\!\bigl(\bm\mu,(\sigma^{(s)})^{2}\bm I\bigr)$
		\EndFor
		\State $\mathcal D_{\text{syn}}\gets\mathcal D_{\text{syn}}\cup\{\bar{\bm{x}}_v^{(0)}\}$
		\EndFor
		\State $\mathcal{D}_\text{aug}\!\leftarrow\!\mathcal{D}_\text{real}\cup\mathcal{D}_\text{syn}$
		\Statex \textbf{Stage 3: Fine-tune Trustworthy Multimodal LLM}
		\State Initialize ${\bm{x}}_g\!\leftarrow\!\bar{\bm{x}}_v$
		\For{epoch $=1$ \textbf{to} $N_\mathrm{llm}$} \Comment{Training epochs $N_\mathrm{llm}$}
		\ForAll{$(\bm{x}_{g},\bm{y}_{g})\!\sim\!\mathcal{D}_\text{aug}$}
		\State $\bm{x}_{pt}\leftarrow\textsc{AlignBlock}(\bm{x}_{g})$
		\State $\bm{\hat y}_{g}\leftarrow\textsc{TMLLM}(\bm{x}_{pt})$
		\State $\bm{\theta}_\mathrm{ts}^*, \bm{\theta}_\mathrm{llm}^*\leftarrow \operatorname{LoRA}(\bm{\hat y}_{g}, \bm{y}_{g})$
		\EndFor
		\EndFor
		\State \Return $\bm{\theta}_\mathrm{d}^*$,  $\bm{\theta}_\mathrm{ts}^*$, $\bm{\theta}_\mathrm{llm}^*$
	\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\begin{algorithm}[t]
	\caption{Inference Pipeline Based on Key Token-Driven Uncertainty Reweighting Strategy}
	\label{alg:inference}
	\begin{algorithmic}[1]
		\Require Test set $\mathcal{D}_\text{test}$, window length $L_{\mathrm{win}}$, weights of TMLLM $\bm{\theta}_\mathrm{ts}^*$ , $\bm{\theta}_\mathrm{llm}^*$, calibration coefficient $\omega$, key-token index $\delta-1$, uncertainty threshold $\psi$
		\ForAll{$\bm{x}_\mathrm{test}\!\sim\!\mathcal{D}_\mathrm{test}$}
		\State $\hat{\bm{y}}_\mathrm{test} \gets \mathrm{TMLLM}(\bm{\theta}_\mathrm{ts}^*$, $\bm{\theta}_\mathrm{llm}^*;\bm{x}_\mathrm{test})$
		\State $\hat{\bm{y}}_\mathrm{test}=\{\mathit{Token}_1,\dots,\mathit{Token}_{N_t}\}$
		\Statex \textbf{Stage 1: Token-Level Normalization}
		\For{$k = 1$ \textbf{to} $N_t$}
		\State $\operatorname{NLL}_k \gets -\!\log P\!\bigl(\mathit{Token}_k \mid \mathit{Token}_{<k}\bigr)$
		\EndFor
		\State $\displaystyle
		\tilde{\operatorname{NLL}}_k \gets 
		\operatorname{NLL}_k \Big/ \sum_{j=1}^{N_t}\operatorname{NLL}_j$
		\Statex \textbf{Stage 2: Key-Token Reweighting}
		\For{$k = 1$ \textbf{to} $N_t$}
		\State $\displaystyle
		\operatorname{UT}_k \gets
		\Bigl[1 + \bigl(\omega r_{\mathrm{pb}} - 1\bigr)\,
		\mathbf{1}_{\{k=\delta-1\}}\Bigr]\!
		\tilde{\operatorname{NLL}}_k$
		\EndFor
		\Statex \textbf{Stage 3: Sequence-Level Aggregation}
		\State $\displaystyle
		\operatorname{UA}\!\bigl(\hat{\bm{y}}_\mathrm{test}\bigr) \gets
		\sum_{k=1}^{N_t}\operatorname{UT}_k$
		\Statex \textbf{Stage 4: Decision}
		\If{$\operatorname{UA}\!\bigl(\hat{\mathbf{y}}_{\text{test}}\bigr)\not\in[\psi_{lower}, \psi_{upper}]$}
		\State \textbf{Reject} $\bm{x}_\mathrm{test}$ \Comment{hallucination detected}
		\Else
		\State \textbf{Output} $\hat{\bm{y}}_\mathrm{test}$
		\EndIf
		\EndFor
	\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

Scenario 1 uses a test set formed by combining the domain-specific subset with UP.
Scenario 2 replaces UP with MaFaulDa, ensuring high semantic similarity to the training dataset.

All evaluations are performed using PyTorch on a workstation equipped with an NVIDIA RTX 4090 GPU.

Eight metrics are utilized for comprehensive evaluation of DA-TMLLM and comparative models, with $\uparrow$ signifying that larger values denote superior performance and $\downarrow$ signifying that lower values are preferred.

\textit{Domain-Specific Accuracy} (DS-ACC, $\uparrow$) compute the ratio of correctly diagnosed fault types to the total number of samples that originate from domain-specific test subset.

\textit{General Accuracy} (G-ACC, $\uparrow$) presents the overall diagnostic accuracy on the mixed test set that combines domain-specific and undesired samples.

\textit{Area under Receiver Operating Characteristic Curve} (AUROC, $\uparrow$) measures separability between domain-specific and undesired samples, higher values showing stronger separation.

\textit{False Alarm Rate at 95\% Recall} (FAR@95, $\downarrow$) is the proportion of domain-specific samples misclassified as undesired samples when the detector achieves 95\% undesired recall.

\textit{Area under the Precision-Recall Curve} (AUPR, $\uparrow$) emphasizes domain-specific accuracy, and higher values indicate cleaner rejection of undesired samples.

\textit{Root Mean Square Error} (RMSE, $\downarrow$) presents generation quality by measuring the quadratic mean of the point-wise differences between the synthesized and actual signal.

\textit{Peak Signal-to-Noise Ratio} (PSNR, $\uparrow$) quantifies reconstruction quality by measuring the ratio between the maximum signal power and the power of the corrupting noise.

\textit{Frequency Spectrum Cosine Similarity} (FSCS, $\uparrow$) expresses generation quality between the synthesized and actual signal by computing the similarity of magnitude spectrum.

We evaluate the hallucination-resilient fault diagnosis performance of DA-TMLLM and the eleven baseline models on the MBHM, MaFaulDa, and UP dataset. Table~\ref{tab:comparison result1} demonstrates the results for scenario 1, 2, and 3 using five metrics, including DS-ACC, G-ACC, AUROC, FAR@95, and AUPR. The experimental results underscore the superiority of DA-TMLLM, which substantially and consistently outperforms all baseline models. In Scenario 1, our model improves DS-ACC and G-ACC by 4.05\% and 5.51\% over the second-best model while achieving ideal detection performance. This dominance is maintained in the complex mixed-domain setting of Scenario 3, where it again secures state-of-the-art results across all metrics. The single exception is in Scenario 2, where DA-TMLLM is marginally second to WDSN on the AUPR metric. Nevertheless, it still leads in G-ACC and delivers a 98.95\% FAR@95 reduction from the closest baseline. Averaging a 6.85\% improvement in G-ACC and a 99.65\% reduction in FAR@95 against the strongest competitors, DA-TMLLM effectively demonstrates its capacity for concurrently performing high-accuracy fault diagnosis and robust hallucination detection. In contrast, baseline models exhibit limitations. VQVAE, DCGAN, QSCGAN, and BearLLM, lacking explicit hallucination detection capability, cannot reliably distinguish between domain-specific and undesired inputs, evidenced by a sharp degradation from DS-ACC to G-ACC and poor AUROC and AUPR scores. Among baselines with detection capabilities, a performance trade-off can be observed. The CDLMs, including MLMM, WDSN, and MDCC, generally prove more adept at hallucination detection. Conversely, LLM-based approaches, including LLMCheck, MIND, SHalluDetect, and LLM-OOD, demonstrate superior in-distribution classification. Both categories tend to misclassify valid domain-specific samples as undesired while being overly conservative in processing inputs flagged as hallucinations.

The ROC curve for DA-TMLLM consistently occupies the ideal top-left corner across all scenarios, signifying superior separability, which aligns with its AUROC and FAR@95 scores. Similarly, the PR curves show our model positioned closest to the top-right corner, maintaining exceptionally high precision across the recall spectrum and confirming its AUPR values. In stark contrast, the curves for baseline models are substantially inferior, reinforcing the conclusion that DA-TMLLM achieves more robust and reliable hallucination detection.

\subsection{消融实验}\label{subsec:DA-TMLLM_ablation_study}

We conduct ablation studies to quantify the contribution of each principal component of DA-TMLLM.
As presented in Table \ref{tab:ablation_study}, experimental results of the ablation study across the three scenarios in terms of DS-ACC, G-ACC, AUROC, FAR@95, and AUPR are reported.

（）{w/o ControlNet} We substitute the ControlNet module with a Wide-Kernel Deep Convolutional Neural Network, thereby removing the conditional guidance and reverting the model to a conventional denoising diffusion probabilistic model. This variant exhibits a decrease in performance across all three scenarios, indicating that the ControlNet is essential for extracting discriminative features from vibration signals under various fault types and working conditions.

（）{w/o CGCDM} We remove the proposed data ControlNet-guided cross-dataset conditional diffusion module and train the diagnostic model exclusively on the original, imbalanced fault signals. This results in a degradation in diagnostic accuracy. This finding underscores the efficacy of our diffusion-based fault mode augmentation, highlighting its effectiveness in mitigating statistical bias and rectifying the distorted decision boundaries caused by data imbalance.

（）{w/o KTURS} We replace the proposed token-level uncertainty, as defined in Eq.~\eqref{equa:token_level_uncertainty}, with the normalized energy score in Eq.~\eqref{equa:normalized_energy_score}. We observe a decline in the performance of undesired input identification, as measured by AUROC, FAR@95, and AUPR. This outcome demonstrates that the designed key-token uncertainty reweighting strategy is effective for detecting sequence-level hallucination.

（）{w/o TMLLM} We remove the trustworthy multimodal large language module and thus eliminate the framework's hallucination detection capability, leading to a decline in the performance of undesired input identification. This outcome confirms that the proposed approach effectively quantifies uncertainties and provides trustworthy diagnostic answers.

This experiment investigates the impact of the diffusion model's noise scheduler $\zeta$, the diagnostic signal length $L_{f}$, the LoRA fine-tuning rank $r_{LoRA}$, and the key-token calibration coefficient $\omega$. The influence of these parameters is evaluated by the RMSE between generated and real signals, ControlNet's diagnostic accuracy on known classes, the binary cross-entropy loss at the final tuning epoch, and the detection rate of undesired inputs, respectively.

（）{Impact of $\zeta$} We evaluate linear, cosine, and sigmoid noise schedulers and find a linear approach yields superior signal reconstruction across most fault types, as shown in Fig.~\ref{fig:Sensitivity_Analysis}. Its uniform noise addition aligns better with the inductive biases for fault signal reconstruction, while non-linear schedulers can impair generative stability.

（）{Impact of $L_{f}$} We investigate the effect of diagnostic signal length on DA-TMLLM's performance by testing values from \{960, 1200, 1600, 2400, 4800\}. As illustrated in Fig.~\ref{fig:Sensitivity_Analysis}, diagnostic accuracy improves with signal length. Shorter signals lack sufficient discriminative information, while excessively long signals can create a GPU memory bottleneck during the diffusion process. Consequently, we select 
$L_{f} = 2400$ to balance diagnostic efficacy against computational overhead.

（）{Impact of $r_{LoRA}$} Since the settings of LoRA influences text-signal pair alignment during fine-tuning, we conduct an experiment targeting the Value Projection ($v\_proj$), Query Projection ($q\_proj$), and All Linear layers ($all\_linear$). The trainable parameters for these modules are 0.0130\%, 0.0223\%, and 0.300\% of the model's total, respectively. We select the LoRA rank $r_{LoRA}$ from \{1, 2, 4, 8, 16\}. As shown in Fig.~\ref{fig:Sensitivity_Analysis}, text-signal alignment performance improves with both trainable parameter count and LoRA rank. The lowest loss of $0.0240$ is achieved when targeting All Linear layers with $r_{LoRA} = 4$, consistent with the optimal parameters in Table~\ref{tab:Hyperparameter}.

（）{Impact of $\omega$} We assess the impact of the uncertainty calibration coefficient $\omega$ over the range \{1, 2, $\cdots$, 50\} on undesired input detection. As shown in Fig.~\ref{fig:Sensitivity_Analysis}, a larger coefficient enhances DA-TMLLM's ability to detect undesired tokens. This occurs because it more accurately quantifies unbiased token-level uncertainty, thereby amplifying the distributional divergence between domain-specific and undesired tokens.

\subsection{故障信号生成质量评估}\label{subsec:DA-TMLLM_signal_generation_quality_evaluation}

We evaluate the quality of synthetic vibration signals generated by DA-TMLLM in comparison with three augmentation-based baselines, including VQVAE, DCGAN, and QSCGAN, for the minority classes of the domain-specific datasets. As summarized in Table \ref{tab:comparison_result2}, DA-TMLLM achieves the lowest RMSE and the highest PSNR and FSCS. Quantitatively, relative to the second-best performing model in each case, DA-TMLLM reduces the average RMSE by 86.9\% while increasing the average PSNR and FSCS by 97.1\% and 67.2\%, respectively, which validates its superiority in data augmentation. This superior performance is attributed to our conditional diffusion model, which excels at capturing complex, non-stationary signal characteristics through a guided, progressive denoising process. In contrast, DCGAN and QSCGAN are prone to training instability, resulting in an incomplete capture of the true data distribution, while VQVAE tends to generate overly smooth signals that lack critical high-frequency details.

\section{本章小结}\label{sec:DA-TMLLM_conclusion}

In this paper, We have proposed DA-TMLLM, a novel method to detect and alleviate hallucinations of diagnostic LLMs.
First, we have designed a ControlNet-guided cross-dataset conditional diffusion model to alleviate statistical biases by injecting controllable semantic guidance and synthesizing diverse samples.
Second, we have designed TMLLM to enhance hallucination detection performance by targeting a key token through point-biserial correlation analysis and significance testing, as well as designing a key token-driven uncertainty reweighting strategy.
Third, we have designed a hallucination-resilient scheme, which integrates the diffusion-based augmentation and TMLLM into a unified pipeline for hallucination detection and alleviation.
Evaluation results obtained from eleven fault-diagnosis datasets have indicated that DA-TMLLM consistently achieves superior performance over eleven comparison models across eight metrics.
